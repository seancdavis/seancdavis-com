Over the last five or six months, something shifted for me. I changed roles, started building with AI, and [found a spark I hadn't felt in years](/posts/obsession-returning-to-the-flow-state-of-2014/). I went absolutely ham. Building all the time. Couldn't stop. Got obsessed.

And it was amazing. But it was also a lot. The world is on fire, I'm doing more than ever, and there's constantly more demanded from every corner of life. Fun? Yes. Sustainable? I wasn't sure.

So I've been rethinking how I work on side projects and put out content. And I'm making a shift.

## AI elevated humans — it didn't replace them

I've spent the better part of this year building with AI agents, [running multiple projects in parallel](/posts/finding-balance-with-context-switching/), standing up custom agent teams, trying to push the boundaries of what's possible. And one of the clearest things I've learned is this: **AI hasn't diminished the value of a human. It's elevated it.**

I know that's not the popular take right now. A lot of leaders are reacting to AI by saying, _great, we can get the same output with fewer people, less cost_. And that frustrates the hell out of me. The right move isn't to cut humans — it's to elevate the work that humans do. If AI can do someone's job, that job needs to change and grow. The task might disappear, but the role should evolve.

And look — just like the courses that sell you the ability to make a million dollars a day, the "fully autonomous AI" narrative is 99% bullshit. You _can_ do more with AI. Absolutely. But it's a tool for humans to use. It's a pattern-matching algorithm that works faster across multiple threads than a human brain can. There's a ton of value in that. But I'm still a strong proponent of a human in the loop — not out of fear, but from experience.

## Social media is a losing game (for me)

The other realization has been about social media — Twitter, specifically. Over the last year, there was this mass exodus from Twitter to Bluesky in the developer world. And for a lot of folks, Bluesky stuck. But the AI crowd? Mostly back on Twitter. And Twitter is a fucking cesspool.

I've spent more time and energy tweeting than I ever have in my entire career. I've built tools to help make it easier. And I've gotten _less_ interaction than ever before. The ratio has completely plummeted.

Here's what I've come to believe: building a real audience on social media — one that actually pays off — has no substitute for time. And I don't mean posting three times a day. I mean the people who genuinely have influence are spending _hours and hours and hours_ on it every single day.

I find that unhealthy. I think the negatives of social media far outweigh the positives, particularly for young people. And I think there are stronger, more impactful ways to build community and connection — even with technology. Better for our brains. Better for our human selves.

## I can't build ten things at the same time

With AI, you _could_ probably spin up 100 products. But not with enough care and attention to make them meaningfully better than what someone else can build. Not all at once.

What I really want is to have a meaningful impact on the creator community — and do it in a way where I feel balanced in my life and can also make a living. Those three things together are what I need to be successful. And that means I can't be all over the place.

At the same time, I still want to move fast. I'm still deeply motivated and inspired by AI. There's so much to explore. So the answer isn't going into a hole for a month — but it's also not spinning up a dozen half-baked projects simultaneously.

## Weekly themes, YouTube-first, social as a megaphone

So here's the shift. I'm going to be much more structured about how I spend my side-project time.

### One theme per week

Every week will have a theme and a goal. I'll spend a little time each day building in line with that theme, and the building will inform the content. At least three days a week, I'll spend time recording educational material on what I've built or learned.

### YouTube as the primary channel

The majority of my content focus is going into video — creating for my YouTube channel, then distributing what makes sense to the blog and other formats. The output will likely be some combination of video, short-form content, and long-form writing.

### Social media as the megaphone

Instead of trying to _win_ at social media, I'm going to use it as a megaphone for the work I'm already doing. Where it makes sense, I'll use it as an interactive engagement tool. But my primary focus won't be scrolling curated feeds and trying to game the algorithm.

## Building the machine first

This framework is designed to let me move fast and stay fluid on a week-to-week basis while remaining focused. It leaves enough space for the little things that come up and keeps the majority of my day on my day job — because this is my passion project, not my primary income.

I'm kicking this off next week. And the first theme is meta: putting in place the tools I need to execute on this whole creation, capture, recording, editing, and distribution workflow. I'll look at what I've already built and what I need to stand up or adjust.

The goal by end of next week is to come away with a working system I can put into practice the following week — which will likely be about establishing a new brand to house all of this work.

Here's to a new era. Time to start building again.
